My Projects

Mechanisms of active sensing

Distinguishing sensory signals that originate from the external world from those caused by our own movements is a fundamental challenge for the nervous system. However, it is still largely unknown how this occurs, particularly at the cellular and circuit level. This task is especially important when an animal moves its sensors to gain information from the environment (think fumbling around for your keys in a bag), a behavior known as ‘active sensing’. I am developing the Drosophila antennal mechanosensory and motor system as a model to study mechanisms of active sensing with cellular resolution, using a combination of whole cell electrophysiology, imaging, genetics, quantitative behavior and machine learning tools.

Central encoding of wind direction

Wind information is used by a wide variety of animals to guide navigation, including most insects. To understand how this sensory cue is computed in the brain, I identified and and characterized novel wind direction encoding circuits in the central brain of the fruit fly. I used a combination of electrophysiology, imaging, pharmacology, modeling, and quantitative behavioral measurements to tackle this problem.

Suver., M.P., Matheson, A.M.M., Sarkar, S., Damiata, M., Schoppik, D. and K.I. Nagel, 2019. “Encoding of wind direction by central neurons in Drosophila.” Neuron 102(4): 828-842.

Baker, K., Dickinson, M.H., Findley, T., Gire, D., Louis, M., Suver, M.P., Verhagen, J., Nagel, K.I. and M. Smear, 2018. “Algorithms for olfactory search across species.” Journal of Neuroscience 38(44): 9383-9389.

Through the bottleneck: descending visual interneurons encode self-motion in Drosophila

How is sensory information integrated and ultimately sent towards motor control centers to guide behavior? In many animals, this must happen through a bottleneck. For example, in flies, a few hundred descending neurons relay information from the central brain to motor centers in the thoracic ganglia. It was previously unclear how state-dependent changes in sensory systems, like those I characterized in the study above, affect downstream circuits. To address this, I described three pairs of visual descending interneurons in the fruit fly, identified their presynaptic partners, and characterized their encoding properties during flight behavior. I then traced the flight-dependent changes in activity in these neurons towards motor centers that control flight. As part of this study, we developed an open-source machine vision tracking system to monitor motor output of the fly during behavior. This work showed how motion information is transformed as it moves from sensory to motor centers.

Suver, M.P., Huda, A., Iwasaki, N., Safarik, S. and Dickinson, M.H., 2016. An array of descending visual interneurons encoding self-motion in Drosophila. Journal of Neuroscience 36(46): 11768-11780.

Behavioral neuromodulation of visual interneurons

Fruit flies control a remarkable repertoire of behaviors with a relatively small brain (on the order of ~100,000 neurons). Through the use of neuromodulators, the brain can perform a greater range of computations using the same neurons and circuits. To investigate how this takes place in the brain of intact, behaving animals, I studied the role of state-dependent modulation in the visual system of Drosophila. I pinpointed a set of neuromodulatory (octopamine) neurons whose activity was necessary and sufficient to induce a flight-dependent change in visual processing. This work provided the first evidence that endogenous release of octopamine modulates visual motion processing in flies.

Suver, M.P., Mamiya, A. and Dickinson, M.H., 2012. Octopamine neurons mediate flight-induced modulation of visual processing in Drosophila.. Current Biology, 22(24), pp.2294-2302.
