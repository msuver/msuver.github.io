My Projects

Central encoding of wind direction

Wind information is used by a wide variety of animals to guide navigation, including most insects. How is this cue represented in the brain? To understand how insects compute wind information, I am identifying and characterizing novel wind direction encoding circuits in the central brain of the fruit fly. I use a combination of electrophysiology, imaging, pharmacology, modeling, and quantitative behavioral measurements to tackle this problem.

Suver., M.P., Matheson, A.M.M., Sarkar, S., Damiata, M., Schoppik, D. and K.I. Nagel, 2019. “Encoding of wind direction by central neurons in Drosophila.” Neuron 102(4): 828-842.

Baker, K., Dickinson, M.H., Findley, T., Gire, D., Louis, M., Suver, M.P., Verhagen, J., Nagel, K.I. and M. Smear, 2018. “Algorithms for olfactory search across species.” Journal of Neuroscience 38(44): 9383-9389.

Through the bottleneck: descending visual interneurons encode self-motion in Drosophila

How is sensory information integrated and ultimately sent towards motor control centers to guide behavior? In insects, this must happen through a bottleneck: a few hundred descending neurons relay information from the central brain to motor centers in the thoracic ganglia. It is also not clear how state-dependent changes in sensory systems, like those I characterized in the study above, affect downstream circuits. To understand this, I described three pairs of visual descending interneurons in the fruit fly, identified their presynaptic partners, and characterized their encoding properties during flight behavior. I then traced the flight-dependent changes in activity in these neurons towards motor centers that control flight. As part of this study, we developed an open-source machine vision tracking system to monitor motor output of the fly during behavior.

Suver, M.P., Huda, A., Iwasaki, N., Safarik, S. and Dickinson, M.H., 2016. An array of descending visual interneurons encoding self-motion in Drosophila. Journal of Neuroscience 36(46): 11768-11780.

Behavioral neuromodulation of visual interneurons

Fruit flies control a remarkable repertoire of behaviors with a relatively small brain (on the order of ~100,000 neurons). How do they make efficient use of their compact brain? One way in which organisms might optimize sensory systems is through the use of neuromodulators. I studied the role of state-dependent modulation in the visual system of Drosophila, and pinpointed a set of neuromodulatory neurons whose activity was necessary and sufficient to induce a flight-dependent change in activity. This work provided the first evidence that endogenous release of octopamine modulates visual motion processing in flies.

Suver, M.P., Mamiya, A. and Dickinson, M.H., 2012. Octopamine neurons mediate flight-induced modulation of visual processing in Drosophila.. Current Biology, 22(24), pp.2294-2302.
